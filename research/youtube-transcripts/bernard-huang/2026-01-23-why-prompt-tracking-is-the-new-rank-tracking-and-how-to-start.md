## Original Content
For years, SEO has been about one core
question. Where do we rank on Google?
Position one, page one, top three.
Entire teams, tools, and dashboards have
been built around answering that single
question. But something fundamental has
changed. Today, your customers aren't
digging through 10 blue links. They're
asking chatbt, Gemini, and other AI
chatbots a question and receiving one
synthesized answer. That shift breaks
the entire idea of traditional rank
tracking.
In the old world, users did the digging.
They searched, they scanned results,
they clicked multiple links, they
compared sources, then they made a
decision. Search was designed around
human limits. Limited time, limited
attention, limited patience. In the new
world, the AI does the digging. AI
doesn't skim 10 results. It doesn't stop
at page one. It doesn't get tired. It
reads dozens, sometimes hundreds of
sources. It evaluates trust, relevance,
authority, and consistency, then
synthesizes a single response. That
response is built on mentions and
citations. And here's the critical
shift. If you're not mentioned in the
answer or cited as a source, you haven't
just lost rank. You've disappeared.
There is no page two. There is no
scrolling. There is no alternative
option. Here's what that looks like in
practice. Imagine you rank number one on
Google for best project management
software. You've done everything right.
Strong content, high-quality backlinks,
solid on page optimization. Now, a user
asks an AI, "What's the best project
management software for agencies
managing climate work?" The AI scans
dozens of sources and instead of
mentioning you, it references two
competitors that rank lower in search
because they speak directly to agency
workflows, are consistently mentioned
across reviews, and clearly matched the
use case. To the user, you don't exist
even though you rank first.
Traditional rank tracking was built for
a different reality. It was designed to
measure visibility in a world where
results were consumed as ranked lists.
Attention was distributed across links
and choice happened after clicking and
comparing. In that world, rankings were
a reliable proxy for visibility. AI
generated answers on the other hand
changed the unit of measurement
entirely. Because AI doesn't surface
lists, it synthesizes conclusions. That
means it doesn't care where you rank. It
cares about whether you comprehensively
answer the question and you appear
consistently trustworthy across sources.
Rankings measure visibility to humans.
AI answers measure trust across the web.
That's why obsessing over rankings alone
is increasingly misleading because the
real question isn't where do we rank,
it's are we showing up in the answer at
all.
This leads to a new way of thinking
about visibility. For years, we've asked
one question, where do we rank? That
question made sense when users typed
keywords into search engines. But AI
changes the input. Users don't search,
they ask questions. And instead of
results, they receive answers. So the
more important question becomes, are we
part of the answer? That's what AI
visibility really means. It's not about
position. It's not about clicks. It's
about inclusion. Whether your brand,
content, or expertise is actually used
by the AI. And once you see visibility
this way, you also realize something
else. You need a new way to measure it.
This is where prompt tracking comes in.
Prop tracking is simply the practice of
monitoring whether your brand shows up
through mentions or citations when AI is
asked the questions your customers
actually use. Here's a basic way to
start tracking prompts today. Start with
a Google sheet and create four columns.
date, prompt, cited, yes or no,
mentioned, yes or no. Each week, take
your top three prompts, the real
conversational questions your customers
would ask AI before making a decision.
Then run each prompt in a temporary chat
with your AI of choice. For each
response, log two things. One, were you
mentioned anywhere in the response? And
two, were you explicitly cited as a
source? The key is consistency. Use the
same model each time and don't change
the wording between tests. That's it. No
scoring, no waiting, no overthinking.
Over time, this creates a trend line
that rankings can't show you. Are you
gaining AI visibility? Are you losing
it? Or are your competitors slowly
replacing you as a recommended source?
This exercise quickly exposes things
traditional SEO hides. You might
discover pages that rank well but never
get cited, brands with weaker rankings
that dominate AI answers, and topics
where you thought you were strong but
never appear. Rate tracking tells you
where you show up. Prop tracking tells
you whether you're trusted. And once you
see that difference is hard to unsee.
Here's the catch. This workflow actually
works. And the more seriously you take
it, the more insight you gain, right up
until it breaks. Because the moment you
try to track more than a few prompts,
cover more than one topic, or check
results consistently over time, the
process becomes cumbersome. What started
as a simple spreadsheet turns into
constant manual work.
The problem becomes obvious once you try
this method and especially when you try
to scale. Manual prompt tracking is time
consuming, is inconsistent, relies on
guesswork about which prompts actually
matter, doesn't scale beyond a handful
of prompts, and breaks the moment you
stop paying attention. Most teams try it
once or twice, get a glimpse of the
insight, and then abandon it. Not
because it isn't useful, but because
it's not sustainable. The framework is
right, the execution is the bottleneck.
